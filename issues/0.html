Employer Stop-Loss Market Intelligence

1Carrier earnings and underwriting posture: margin protection remains the theme

What Changed

  • HM Insurance Group (Highmark) disclosed a sharp swing in its stop-loss results, reporting a 2025 operating loss of $107M on $1.3B revenue versus a 2024 operating profit of $56M on $1.2B revenue, attributing the deterioration to “an increase in the frequency and severity of high-dollar claims.” (BenefitsPro)
  • Voya’s Employee Benefits segment highlighted improved underwriting margins in Stop Loss and explicitly framed in-force premium declines as a deliberate tradeoff to protect margins via “pricing discipline and enhanced risk selection within the Stop Loss business.” (Voya)

Why It Matters

These disclosures reinforce a 2026 market reality: even when financial statements look improved (e.g., underwriting margin improvement), carrier strategy may still be to protect margin over growth, which tends to show up as stricter terms, higher attachment points, narrower participation in distressed segments, and reduced willingness to “buy” business via aggressive quoting.

2Level-funded regulatory attention: NAIC working groups drafting guidance

What Changed

NAIC working groups signaled increased focus on level-funded plans—often described as a hybrid structure combining self-insurance with low-limit stop-loss—with the ERISA and Alternative Health Coverage Working Group initiating a project to draft level-funded plan guidance for regulators. (BenefitsPro)

Why It Matters

Level-funded growth expands the addressable market for stop-loss but also heightens regulatory scrutiny over the economic substance of the arrangement (e.g., whether risk transfer is too thin, marketing practices, and consumer protection outcomes). If states tighten rules via bulletins or enforcement under existing authority, carriers/TPAs may need to adjust minimum specific/aggregate levels, disclosure regimes, and employer education—potentially impacting quote competitiveness and persistency.

Regulatory reference point (attachment point floors)

NAIC maintains a Spring 2025 chart of state stop-loss coverage requirements, including examples of minimum employer retention thresholds (e.g., $20,000 per individual in the NAIC model reference) and aggregate minimums tied to expected claims. (NAIC stop-loss coverage chart (PDF))

3PBM reform: new contract and reporting expectations for self-insured ERISA plans

What Changed

NFP summarized provisions in the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026 (CAA 2026), including mandatory rebate pass-through (100% of rebates/fees/other remuneration to the plan sponsor for self-insured ERISA plans, generally quarterly), plus drug-level reporting to sponsors of large self-insured plans and audit rights. (NFP)

Why It Matters

For stop-loss and captive programs, PBM reforms can be a second-order underwriting and risk management lever: improved rebate transparency and pass-through requirements may reduce net pharmacy trend volatility, but transition costs (contract renegotiation, reporting systems, and compliance governance) can create short-term disruption. From a stop-loss perspective, anything that changes specialty drug net cost trajectories (or creates utilization shifts) can move large-claim incidence for pharmacy-heavy claimants.

4GLP-1 utilization management: employer guardrails are becoming the norm

What Changed

Sequoia reported that GLP-1 coverage discussions are a top benefits agenda item for 35% of employers, with approximately a quarter of employers covering GLP-1s for weight management overall (48% among employers with 1,000+ employees), and noted that 85% of employers use some form of guardrail (most commonly prior authorization at 67%). (Sequoia)

Why It Matters

GLP-1s remain an underwriting and captive feasibility variable because they can shift both: (1) high-cost claimant probability (through downstream metabolic improvements) and (2) near-term paid claims due to high unit cost and demand. The fact pattern here is increasingly not “cover or exclude,” but “cover with guardrails,” meaning stop-loss carriers and captive actuaries may want to explicitly model utilization controls as plan design variables rather than treating GLP-1 coverage as a binary.

5Market structure watch: tightening language around rates and terms

Tokio Marine HCC’s market commentary continues to emphasize a market tightening on rates and terms, driven by rising claim frequency and insurer responses such as stricter terms and rate increases. (Tokio Marine HCC)

Why It Matters

Even without a single headline “rate filing,” consistent carrier messaging can foreshadow quote behavior: disciplined underwriting, fewer concessions, and more conservative corridor/aggregate structures. For captive programs, this can accelerate interest in alternative risk transfer where employers can tolerate volatility.

Key dates / watchlist (next 30–60 days)

  • Track follow-up outputs from NAIC working groups (ERISA and Alternative Health Coverage Working Group; Consumer Information Working Group) related to level-funded guidance. (BenefitsPro)
  • PBM reform implementation planning: CAA 2026 provisions are described as generally effective for plan years beginning 30 months after enactment (often January 1, 2029 for calendar-year plans), while DOL’s proposed PBM fee disclosure rule is described as proposed for plan years beginning on or after July 1, 2026. (NFP)
